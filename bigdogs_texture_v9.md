KEY CONTEXT — what makes this challenge distinct

This is an adoption-campaign challenge for St. Hubert's that uses the science of domestication and selective breeding as the substrate for persuasive narrative writing. The cognitive turn the challenge orbits is the gap between the photo assumption families make about a large dog and the dog they actually meet. Closing that gap is the job of the profile card. The challenge ends when families walking the adoption kennels read the cards.

The cohort is 5/6 only. The science (MS-LS4 selective breeding, domestication, polygenic inheritance) is on-level for the grade-6 half and a deliberate reach-up for the grade-5 half. The math (6.RP.A.2 unit rates, 6.RP.A.3 misleading comparisons, 6.SP.B.4 graphs, 6.SP.B.5 summarizing data) sits at the top of the 5/6 band. The ELA (W.AW.6.1, W.NW.6.3, W.WR.6.5, W.SE.6.6, W.WP.6.4, RI.AA.6.7, SL.PE.6.1, SL.PI.6.4, SL.UM.6.5) is grade-6 across the board.

THE PLACE — named, recurring

THE AUDIENCE — named

Families walking the adoption kennels at St. Hubert's. This is the reader the cards are written for. Card voice, vocabulary, register, and content selection all serve this reader. Not "the public," not "people," not "adopters." Families walking the kennels.

For Act 6, the secondary audience is St. Hubert's staff: the cohort briefs the partner on what they learned about the adoption gap, the stories they created, and the recommendations they would make beyond the cards.

THE ARTIFACT — named, recurring

Narrative profile cards. One card per dog. Printed and clipped to the dog's kennel. Each card turns one real dog's history and observed behavior into a short, honest story that reframes the size that made adopters hesitate.

Recurring sub-artifacts built across the arc that feed the card:
- The photo assumption: a written prediction of what someone would assume about a dog from its photo alone (Act 3).
- The observation log: a structured record of what the dog actually does, kept separate from what students conclude (Act 3).
- The adoption-gap claim: a data-backed written claim about whether a real gap exists, drawn from rates and hidden-factor analysis (Act 1).
- The hypothesis: a testable statement about why big dogs are passed over (Act 4).
- The survey: non-leading questions plus a sampling design plus cleaned and visualized responses (Act 4).
- The seminar defense: question-and-defend the cohort's claims against database evidence on bites, fatalities, cost, housing, and breed energy levels (Act 4).
- The draft profile card, peer-reviewed (Act 5).
- The final card and the partner briefing (Act 6).

THE ARC — six Acts, in order

Act 1. Is This Even True? Driving question: "Is this even true?" Students open on one real long-stay dog at the shelter. They learn the difference between counts and rates and use rate reasoning to check whether the long-stay-big-dog impression is a real adoption gap or a hidden-factor artifact. Evidence produced: initial analysis of adoption data and a written claim about whether a real adoption gap exists.

Act 2. How a Wolf Became Both a Great Dane and a German Shepherd. Driving question: "How did dogs become dogs?" Students learn inheritance, variation, domestication, natural selection, selective breeding, and polygenic inheritance through the wolf-to-dog story. The teaching uses two named hooks. The Siberian silver fox experiment, told aloud by the guide: humans bred wild foxes for tameness across 60 years and accidentally produced floppy ears, wagging tails, and spotted coats. The border collie reading: a short profile of sheepdogs clearing a field in under a minute, with puppies herding at six weeks, ties "bred for a job" back to wolves and the herding-as-toned-down-hunting connection. Evidence produced: explanation of how domestication and selective breeding produced modern dog breeds.

Act 3. Meet Your Dog. Driving question: "What's your dog actually like?" Each student is assigned one real big dog and first sees only a photo. They write a photo assumption. Then they meet the actual dog and observe. The skill is keeping observation and inference separate. The guide read-aloud hook is Alexandra Horowitz on a dog smelling the passage of time. Evidence produced: a written assumption based on the dog's photo, a structured observation log, and a comparison of the two.

Act 4. Why Do People Pass Them By? Driving question: "Why do people pass them by?" Students commit to a hypothesis, gather evidence through surveys and database research, and test common beliefs against facts: are big dogs actually more dangerous, do they cost dramatically more, do they need more space. Students learn the difference between assumptions, opinions, and evidence. Evidence produced: survey findings, evidence checks, and conclusions supported by data.

Act 5. Telling the Truth So It Moves Someone. Driving question: "How do stories move people?" Students turn observations into stories. They learn how specific moments reveal character and how evidence creates trust. They draft, test on a reader, and revise. Two mentor texts are named. "Eddie the Terrible," a wildly honest adoption listing titled "Three Reasons You DON'T Want To Adopt Eddie The Terrible" that worked. "Billie Jean, the dog who smiled," a scared dog adopted after one true detail (the grin she flashed when anyone approached) spread. The two are set side by side as comedy vs. sincerity, asking which is more honest and which works. Evidence produced: draft profile cards and reader feedback.

Act 6. The Cards Go Up. Driving question: "What do we owe the shelter?" Students finalize the cards. The cards are printed and clipped to each dog's kennel. The cohort briefs St. Hubert's, handing off the cards and explaining the thinking behind the campaign and the recommendations beyond the cards themselves. Evidence produced: final profile cards and partner briefing.
