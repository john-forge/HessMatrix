The frame

Two frameworks, complementary rather than competing. Bloom's Taxonomy classifies what kind of cognitive activity is happening, ordered from simpler to more complex: Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create. Webb's Depth of Knowledge classifies how cognitively complex the activity is — Recall and Reproduction, Skills and Concepts, Strategic Thinking, Extended Thinking. Depth is not difficulty: memorizing one hundred vocabulary words is hard but stays at Level 1. Depth lives in reasoning, planning, transfer, evidence, and sustained thinking.

The Hess Cognitive Rigor Matrix crosses the two into a single six-by-four grid because the same Bloom verb can sit at very different depths. "Create" at Depth of Knowledge Level 1 is brainstorming a list. "Create" at Depth of Knowledge Level 4 is synthesizing a multi-source thesis with a real audience. The trap the matrix exists to catch: Bloom's hierarchy makes higher verbs sound prestigious, so a "Create a poster about volcanoes" assignment can read as advanced when students are mostly copying facts onto cardboard — high Bloom verb, low Depth of Knowledge, the appearance of rigor without the substance.
